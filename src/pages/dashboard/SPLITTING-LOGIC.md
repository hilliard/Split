The golden rule of cost-splitting apps is that you always enter the total amount paid at the register (or in this case, on the credit card statement). You let the app do the math.

If John bought the tickets on his card:

John creates a new expense called "Flights to/from Vegas".

He enters the total cost: $593.92.

He selects: Paid by John and Split Equally.

The app automatically takes that $593.92, divides it by 2, and records that Mary owes John $296.96. (Note: $593.92 divided by 2 is exactly $296.96, so your math of $296.81 was off by a few cents!)

Why your proposed method fails
If John goes in and creates an expense for $148.40, he is entering Mary's share as the total cost.

If John enters an expense of $148.40 and clicks "Split Equally," the app thinks the total cost at the airline was only $148.40. It will divide that number by two, meaning the app will tell Mary she only owes John $74.20.

What if you really want to track the two legs separately?
If you are building your own app and want to allow users to itemize the trip, you can enter two expenses, but you still must enter the total cost of each leg.

Assuming the flights cost exactly the same each way ($296.96 per leg):

Expense 1: "Flight TO Vegas" - $296.96 (Paid by John, Split Equally. Mary owes $148.48).

Expense 2: "Flight FROM Vegas" - $296.96 (Paid by John, Split Equally. Mary owes $148.48).

The Bottom Line: Always enter the number that shows up on the credit card receipt. Let the app's algorithm figure out who owes what!
